Thoughts on Human Trafficking

2022-02-24 | 想法

Challenges

Such acts are devoid of statistical representations and therefore more widespread than we thought. They are hard to detect, trace, and be penalized because of their victimization and their ontological meaning. Such crime, like other organizational crimes, presents diffuse, delayed, and less reported victimization. First, its victims cannot be categorized into a specific group or a particular individual. Second, victims will notice their predicament mostly at the end of the whole process (or, sadly speaking, at the end of their lives). Based on the reasons above, victims are less likely to report their victimization to law enforcement agencies or the public. Meanwhile, they would also feel shame to tell their experiences and fear reprisals from stakeholders.

Ontologically speaking, such act itself derives from globalization, originated from the interstate system, dominated by profit, facilitated by industrialization, and secured by external forces. Meanwhile, every state all tries to maximize its national interests by circumventing risks, for example, reservations, declarations, and understandings, which would somehow undermine the original purpose of such protocol so that attenuates its efficacy.

Responses

Besides endeavoring in strengthening the interstate corporation among criminal justice systems and caring for victims, the state shall strive for exalting its “local values" under which people would deserve fair distribution of goods. The major reason for human smuggling is poverty. Western countries represent decent lifestyles. Such notion diffused by globalization triggers them to repudiate their current social-economic status, vexing them and finally provoking them into the risk. Therefore, states themselves and the international society should put more effort into providing equal resources facilitating states with economical disparities. Enhancing their local values to increase their confidence and resistance within the surge of globalization.
